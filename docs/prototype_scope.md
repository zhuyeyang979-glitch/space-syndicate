# Prototype Scope

This Godot build converts the board-game draft into a playable real-time digital sandbox, not a final rules engine.

## Implemented as interactive systems

- The main play screen is now intentionally focused: a large planet map, the anonymous card track, the active player's private hand, one concise next-action hint, and a dismissible early-run checklist for first summon/build/buy/play/economy overview. The checklist shows the current next step as a structured three-part card—action, why it matters, and where to click/which entry to use—and tracks the economy-overview step only when the player actually opens that menu. Debug/status-heavy panels are kept out of the primary UI; role/economy/region details are read through menu branches. Rival cash, hand size, hand faces, and discard records are not shown on the main screen.
- The map can be opened in a fullscreen overlay from the button above the map.
- The world uses a spherical surface model. At low zoom the player sees a globe in space with region boundaries projected onto the visible sphere; at high zoom the same surface is projected into a flat XY-style view.
- Each run randomly partitions the planet into land and ocean regions. Land and ocean both produce terrain-appropriate goods; ocean can provide marine products such as fish, algae, subsea oil, coral materials, or tidal energy while also supporting major shipping routes.
- The main menu opens a 开局准备 branch before a new run, showing 3-8 total seats, 2-7 AI opponent count, roguelike challenge depth, per-player public alien role-card selection, optional random AI roles, independent starter monster card faces, first-summon access, bound-skill rewards, and the monster landing card-buying radius before the player confirms. Each confirmed run starts with no field monsters and resolves role cards without duplicates from a pool of twenty-four selectable alien syndicate roles. Their positive passives are live rules: starting cash, per-minute cashflow from a named city product, a free extra card after buying in a matching region, cash when an owned monster upgrades, private intel reveals/traces, card-owner wager modifiers, contract-flow discounts, expanded card-acquisition range, or public +1 monster/military control limit. Role cards do not modify starter-monster HP/speed/duration/bound-skill count, so public identities do not fingerprint monster ownership. Setup and the role codex show the concrete trigger with procedural temporary card art, while the main game screen keeps only compact hand/cash context. The first summon ignores the normal region/product-flow restriction. Later monster cards respect the active owned-monster limit, can upgrade or refresh owned same-name monsters, and print HP, movement, field duration, and summon-region restriction on their card face/rules; different monsters can require generic, land, or ocean monster-zone landing rules. No monster is persistently player-controlled.
- Legacy four-monster lineup/preselection state and hidden codex controls have been removed from runtime, local settings, and saves. The only setup choice is each player's starter monster card; every field monster thereafter exists because a monster card was played.
- Empty-field starts show a first-summon prompt in the active player's panel, including the current landing district and a direct starter-card play button.
- Monster actions, special actions, failed movement, and major reactions appear as text callouts on the map.
- The player hand uses card-face panels with tags, effect text, strategy-route/use text, field-derived strength budget, action buttons, and procedural temporary card art. The route label and budget are derived from card fields such as economy deltas, route damage/repair, GDP bets, contract terms, intel effects, monster effects, and supply effects.
- Selected districts expose a compact current supply-card choice; cards are acquired with the map/card shortcuts while the full card art remains in the hand and codex.
- Cards are played as one-shot anonymous events rather than charged abilities. District cards are normally bought with player funds only from a monster's current or adjacent region, with an 80% current-region price. Role/card-access effects can extend buying to two-hop or global supply at a higher multiplier, but they do not expand monster-card summon range. A submitted non-persistent card leaves the hand as soon as it enters the anonymous track, then resolves once after its public display. Reacquiring an owned card upgrades it through rank IV. Buying a new normal card at the five-card normal hand limit opens a private discard choice; the public log does not reveal hand size, discarded card, or whether the buyer was forced to discard.
- Card prices use the rank-I effect's power and display explicit base/advanced/high/flagship tiers; all ranks keep their rank-I purchase price. Card details also show a strength budget band, main power drivers, and counterplay/gating points so I-IV upgrades have a visible rationale beyond bigger numbers. Playing a card checks city product flow without consuming it, while a few cards also require an extra cash payment; monster cards can surcharge the summoner based on the number of field monsters already present. Temporary economic effects use real-second duration fields for card data, UI, AI scoring, and tests. Economy overview tracks build spend, card spend, card income, current-refresh cashflow, recent cash movement, and private ledgers instead of keeping those details on the main play screen.
- Economy-card and interaction effects include price pumping, short selling, market stabilization, product-level upgrades, product-line shifts, demand redesign, route sabotage, route repair/insurance/flow acceleration through 应急修复, rival production/transport/consumption suppression through 竞争封锁, private clue pursuit through 线索悬赏, route-weather forecasting through 航线预报, direct player pressure through 星链拆解 and 影仓牵引, temporary city ownership disputes through 产权冻结, broad city suppression through 轨道齐射, product-growth catalysts, sustained product contracts such as 远期采购, 期货套保, and 包销协议, temporary city order/contract deals such as 短期订单, 军需临单, and 星际会展, plus commodity futures, 港仓囤货 stockpiles, and 城市买涨/城市做空 GDP derivative cards. GDP derivative and commodity futures cards use real-time holding windows instead of economy-cycle durations. Warehouse stockpiles leave anonymous public city status and feed event targeting, monster attraction/resource matching, and hidden AI attack/defense pressure. These systems feed back into current prices, volatility, city product/demand lists, disrupted-route penalties, sustained product demand/supply pressure, product growth multipliers, city logistics multipliers, temporary route-flow windows, temporary contract income, region-damage GDP penalties, city-ownership dispute penalties, current GDP/min, and realtime cashflow.
- Military-force cards are now field-driven combat cards in the shared card pool. The prototype includes 行星防卫军 plus differentiated air, land, and ocean assets: 制空战斗机, 轨道轰炸机, 重装坦克, 导弹阵地, 潜航舰队, and 星海战舰. These units are fully controlled through private reusable military-command skills instead of autonomous AI. Deployment and movement use terrain data: air units can operate broadly, tanks and missile batteries prefer land, and submarines/warships prefer ocean. Their movement does not create monster-style building crush damage, but military strikes or armed pressure can add short GDP penalties and route pressure that feed the same city income/GDP trend displays as other economic effects.
- Summoned monsters apply positive economy weather to their preferred products. Some monsters and cards can double or otherwise boost a product's positive price-growth pressure, while logistics cards can speed fulfilled-demand commercial flow for selected owned cities.
- The economy overview keeps a compact recent cash path, tracked-window net change, and recent economy ledger visible alongside cumulative spending and income; the main play screen keeps this out of the hand area.
- Cards that require a monster target pause before queueing and ask the player to choose a target monster; cards that directly affect another player pause for a target-player choice. Contract decisions, target choices, private discard-on-purchase, and the monster-wager UI shell are routed through one reusable temporary decision panel base. All submitted cards then use the same anonymous resolution lane: the first card in an empty lane opens a 0.5-second simultaneous-play window, a lone card proceeds to a 5-second public display, and multiple cards open one 5-second anonymous auction before the whole batch order is locked.
- Anonymous card auctions show public bid amounts without revealing bidders or owners. The locked batch resolves in bid/clockwise order and does not reopen auctions mid-batch. New cards submitted after intake closes or during display enter a separate next-batch waiting area; when the current batch clears, those cards open one combined auction, while a lone waiting card goes directly to display. Locked bids pay privately to the previous resolved card's owner when one exists, including across a batch boundary.
- The top card track records anonymous history/current/pending cards, can be dragged horizontally, and supports owner-guess wagers from the current player. Correct guesses publicly label the card owner and transfer the stake from owner to guesser; wrong guesses privately pay the true owner without revealing the label. The simultaneous-play lobby, full-screen reveal, and track all show the submitted card's play-requirement snapshot as public inference evidence, while resolved history and the economy overview keep anonymous tip-payment clues showing when a locked bid paid the previous anonymous card's owner without exposing player identity.
- Card resolution logs and map callouts are anonymous, including one-shot monster commands, monster-lure cards, and economy effects. Lure cards can redirect a target monster's next automatic movement toward the current selected district, then expire. During live play, only the current player's exact cash, assets, income, cash path, ledger, hand size, hand faces, and discard records are visible; rival economies and rival hands are hidden until final settlement and must be inferred from public clues.
- The economy overview now also summarizes recent monster damage cash clues, including the latest loss, cumulative owner loss, remaining damage-cash pool, source, and whether ownership has become public while leaving unrelated player cash private.
- The economy overview includes a current-player inference board that groups private city annotations, publicly revealed card owners, recent anonymous card play requirements, and publicly revealed monster owners without validating guesses, scanning rival economies, or exposing hidden truth.
- The main menu includes 局势排名, 经济总览, 情报档案, 新手引导, 游戏规则, and a unified 图鉴 branch with submenus for 角色图鉴, 怪兽生态档案, 卡牌图鉴, 商品图鉴, and 区域图鉴. 怪兽牌 belongs to 卡牌图鉴; 怪兽生态档案 is reserved for field-monster behavior, action probabilities, resource preference, and damage data. The root entry layer is organized as reusable section headers plus responsive card grids, so 开局、局势、资料、存档、系统 entries can automatically reflow across 1-3 columns as screen space changes. The menu shell also keeps reusable quick-navigation chips for 开局、局势、经济、情报、规则、图鉴 so players can jump between major branches from any submenu without returning to the root first; the current branch is disabled as a location cue. A reusable interaction-hint strip now sits under the breadcrumb and changes by page state, reminding players about responsive card entries, hover previews, thumbnail albums, double-click detail entry, previous/next detail switching, and return-to-thumbnail paths without hard-coding those hints into every submenu body.
- 卡牌图鉴 is one shared card pool, including monster cards, with monster, economy, business/contract, combat/command, intel, supply/lure, and other filters. It opens as a responsive thumbnail album, supports hover/single-click concise detail preview, uses double-click to enter a card detail page, and keeps previous/next card navigation inside that detail page.
- The menu can save and load the current run locally, including generated map, city ownership, cards, monster state, timers, logs, and scoring state.
- The main menu has no separate monster-selection branch; monster entry is handled by cards after a run begins.
- The read-only monster bestiary opens as a responsive thumbnail album with hover/single-click detail preview, double-click detail entry, detail-only previous/next navigation, and a return-to-thumbnails path. Detail pages show procedural temporary monster art alongside action descriptions, I级/IV级 action probabilities, linked monster-card attributes, fixed skills, resource focus, positive economy weather, movement speed, move-crush damage, resource-drain damage, and knockback-collision damage.
- The card codex shows every card's tags, target requirement, reference price, effect text, strategy route/use text, strength budget/counterplay summary, numeric rule facts, and procedural temporary card art, including temporary contract income, route-flow windows, intel trace counts, weather forecast duration, military unit terrain/deployment/GDP-pressure facts, and real-time duration where relevant.
- The product codex opens as a responsive thumbnail album. Hovering or single-clicking a product shows a preview with price tier, current/recent prices, volatility, supply/demand counts, route disruption pressure, active product contracts, economy weather, and product-filtered city clues; double-clicking opens the detail page, where previous/next navigation and return-to-thumbnails are available.
- The region codex shows public details for each generated region, including terrain, HP/damage, latest damage source, route load, monster attraction reasons, local goods, public city state, temporary contract status, route-flow acceleration, region card choices, and neighboring regions without revealing hidden ownership.
- Players can switch active operator, urbanize districts, save private city-owner guesses, acquire district cards, and play hand cards in realtime.
- The standings menu previews each player's current cash, surviving city assets, potential GDP/min, cumulative business income, and estimated score. During a run it shows public situation changes—anonymous card aftermath, city clues, monster cash clues, weather, and visible map/economy results—while AI route/pressure/scoring buckets remain hidden internal opponent logic. When a run ends, an automatic final settlement page opens with the post-run summary and direct buttons for standings, economy overview, and starting another run.
- The economy overview menu gives a live decision page for product hot/cold rankings, active economy weather, route-income prospects, public situation changes, anonymous clues, and the current player's private cash/assets/income/path/ledger while hiding rival economy totals, rival hands, and AI internal plans until final settlement.
- AI syndicates can automatically and anonymously urbanize profitable empty land during global market refreshes, spending their own funds and creating new unknown-owner buildings for the active player to mark and infer.
- AI syndicates can also perform anonymous commercial actions around the live economy and global refreshes. They spend operating funds to create product demand/supply pressure or sabotage a competing city's route, leaving public city clues without revealing the acting owner. They score hand-card candidates, anonymously play eligible monster/economy/contract/direct-interaction cards, buy cards from accessible monster-supply regions, privately discard low-value normal cards when a purchase would exceed the hand limit, participate in simultaneous-play bidding with budgeted tips, automatically respond to contract signing windows, infer city owners from product clues, wager on anonymous card ownership, use monster-lure cards to steer resource-matching monsters toward high-value competing cities, use direct-interaction cards against likely leaders or high-value hand/city targets, maintain an economic focus product, and switch between grow-focus, defend-routes, and disrupt-competitors strategic intents. Human card-supply windows snapshot access and price when opened, allowing the purchase to finish even if a monster moves away during selection; one player can only keep one such region supply window. Card scoring now includes a generic effect-field layer plus playability and hand-pressure metadata for future cards, including cash, GDP derivative, production/transport/consumption, route damage/repair, route-flow multipliers, intel trace/city reveal/contract trace, weather-control, draw, access, damage, direct hand/city interaction, and market-pressure fields. Each AI also tracks opening/midgame/endgame phase plus leader/contesting/trailing posture, so the same card field can be valued differently when an AI is stabilizing its first city, building a route engine, defending a lead, or trying to sabotage the leader. Each AI also maintains a product-route plan with build-supply, create-demand, strengthen-route, defend-route, and attack-rival stages. New supply/demand/flow progress preserves the active plan, while a score margin prevents random-map noise from causing constant product switching; in create-demand, pure supply cards now receive a stronger stage-mismatch penalty so AI does not abandon a demand gap just because a production card has large raw numbers. Six AI personality profiles currently expose primary development-route preferences that cover six money-facing routes—city growth, contract supply/demand, financial speculation, monster pressure, intel/supply, and direct interaction—so setup and the card codex can show route diversity before a run begins. Build, card, contract, business, bid, route, strategy, and intel candidates plus training samples carry phase/posture/bonus and route-product/stage/score/bonus metadata alongside focus, strategy, target, target owner, target player, generic-effect, playability, hand-pressure, and learned reward metadata. Realtime cashflow/global-refresh rewards and final roguelike money/rank rewards update per-seat learned policy values that influence later choices without leaking between AI seats.
- Generated maps scale by roguelike depth: early depths create small spherical planets with 6-9 irregular land/ocean regions, while deeper runs can reach several dozen regions, currently roughly 40-54 at depth VI, and a higher cash goal. The roguelike target is to earn enough money to survive the current planet and push into bigger planets. Once any player's visible estimated settlement money hits the depth cash goal, a saved anonymous 60-second final countdown begins; final settlement still ranks everyone by money at countdown end. Regions show visible city clusters, hidden ownership, private annotations, damage, business income, trade routes, and local card choices.
- Land and ocean regions initially produce one terrain-appropriate good and have one local demand. Ocean goods include marine food, algae/fiber, subsea oil, coral/shell materials, offshore crystal, and tidal energy. Anonymous contract cards can later add, replace, or remove supply and demand.
- Each city starts with one alien product and one demanded product. Demand routes connect to city or land suppliers, and destroyed transit regions disrupt income.
- Area contract cards require supply and demand endpoints to be selected before play. Their public five-second reveal displays those preselected endpoints and card terms only, then a separate five-second target-owner accept/reject window opens without blocking other card plays; timeout counts as rejection.
- Product prices are refreshed every 30-60 seconds from supply, demand, disrupted routes, sustained product contracts, and any active growth-weather multiplier; those current prices feed production, fulfilled-demand, and transit GDP/min. Route-flow multipliers increase fulfilled-demand logistics GDP while their duration lasts.
- City income now has an explicit breakdown: product revenue, fulfilled-route revenue, transit revenue, permanent bonuses, temporary contract income, competition penalties, route-disruption penalties, region-damage GDP penalties, temporary military pressure, and temporary ownership-dispute penalties. The breakdown is visible in the region codex and economy overview, and global market snapshots also expose a public GDP trend with latest change and a short driver/pressure summary.
- Matching products across rival cities create customer competition and reduce both cities' GDP/min.
- The map can show routes for a selected product, including disrupted routes.
- Monster targeting favors panic, commercial city value, product competition, resource preference, trade-route load, proximity, miasma pressure, and other monsters. Multiple matching resources in one district stack, making it possible for several monsters to converge on the same resource-rich area.
- Movement, pursuit, card range, AOE, and knockback tuning are expressed in meters on the spherical surface. Monster movement crush, resource drain, named combat actions, and knockback collision all feed the shared region HP/destruction track, so cities and trade routes can be damaged by monster travel as well as attacks. Region damage now directly lowers surviving city GDP; flying movement does not apply path-crush damage, while aquatic movement uses ocean/land speed multipliers.

## Deliberate simplifications

- The prototype supports 3-8 total seats with 2-7 AI opponents, but AI intelligence is still first-pass: it now covers global-refresh city expansion, commercial actions, card buying, eligible card play, bidding, contract responses, city-owner inference, card-owner wagering, monster-lure target planning, economic focus, grow/defend/disrupt intent, and staged product-route plans before later work expands into learned long-horizon combinations and final-money reward optimization.
- Card faces and monster portraits now have procedural temporary art; authored final card frames, monster portraits, and map art are still future work.
- Many named cards are represented by reusable effect categories.
- Setup and detailed debugging are intentionally de-emphasized while the primary play screen is being shaped.
- A first-pass tutorial menu is implemented; a fully interactive, step-by-step onboarding flow is still future work.
- The smoke test is intentionally broad rather than exhaustive; card-by-card balance and long-session economic behavior still need dedicated tests after the rules stabilize.
