# Current Prototype Rules Summary

This file summarizes the current Godot prototype rules. `docs/rules_extracted.txt` remains an archival extraction from the older tabletop draft and may contain outdated wording.

## Core premise

Players are alien syndicate operators secretly building commercial city clusters during an autonomous monster war on a randomly generated land/ocean planet. The prototype is moving toward a PVE roguelike structure: each run has 3-8 total seats, usually one human/local seat plus 2-7 AI opponents. Players earn from city businesses and trade routes, protect their own urbanization, and try to destroy rival cities or logistics nodes—especially competitors selling or demanding similar products.

## Current digital rules

- A run starts with no monsters on the planet. Setup chooses 3-8 total seats, 2-7 AI opponents, roguelike challenge depth, one public alien syndicate role card per player, and one independent rank-I starter monster card per player. The first summon ignores the normal region and product-flow restrictions.
- Twenty-one role cards are currently selectable, and a run resolves them without duplicates. Human/local players pick directly; AI seats can be assigned directly or left on random, which resolves to an unused public role at run start. Role passives grant combinations of opening cash, per-minute cashflow from matching city products, a free extra card when buying in a matching product region, cash when an owned monster upgrades, limited private intel reveals/traces, card-owner wager modifiers, contract-flow discounts, and expanded card-acquisition range. Role cards do not modify starter monster HP, speed, field duration, or fixed-skill count, so public identity does not fingerprint monster ownership.
- Later monster cards normally require a monster's current region or an adjacent region as their summon point. There is no field-monster cap.
- Runtime, settings, saves, and the codex UI contain no compatibility-only four-monster lineup or preselection controls. Field monsters come exclusively from played monster cards.
- All monsters are autonomous actors. No monster is persistently player-controlled; cards and bound skills can only direct a specific one-off action.
- Monster cards show rank I-IV, HP, movement speed, movement ecology, field duration, summon restriction, fixed-skill count, product-flow requirement, and any extra cash cost. Their cash surcharge can scale with the number of monsters already on the field. Most monsters leave automatically when their field duration ends even if they were not defeated. Flying movement skips path-crush damage; aquatic movement can be faster through ocean and slower on land.
- Summoning is anonymous and grants the summoner persistent bound skill cards according to the monster-card rank. When an owned monster takes damage, the owner loses money in proportion to that monster's max-HP loss and the resulting cash clue reveals its ownership. The economy overview keeps recent monster cash clues with latest loss, cumulative loss, remaining damage-cash pool, source, and public ownership status without revealing unrelated private cash totals. Takeover cards can secretly change ownership, invalidate the previous owner's bound skills, grant the new owner fresh bound skills, and reset the next damage clue to the new owner.
- Each map is randomly partitioned into land and ocean regions on the spherical world surface. Roguelike depth I-VI scales the planet from a small early board of 6-9 regions to larger worlds with dozens of regions, currently up to roughly 40-54 regions, and raises the cash goal for the run. Roguelike progression is money-shaped: players are trying to earn enough settlement cash to challenge bigger planets. When any player's visible estimated settlement money reaches the cash goal, a saved anonymous 60-second final countdown begins; everyone can still act during that minute, and the final winner is whoever has the most settlement money when it ends.
- Land regions initially produce one good and have one local demand. Ocean regions produce no goods and mainly act as shipping lanes; anonymous contract cards can later add, replace, or remove supply and demand.
- Players spend funds to urbanize empty land districts. Every building cluster is visible on the map, but only its owner sees the true ownership.
- AI syndicates evaluate the live state continuously and also receive a public global market refresh every 30-60 seconds. During those refreshes they may automatically spend funds to urbanize empty high-value land or perform anonymous commercial actions such as adding product demand/supply pressure or sabotaging a competing city's trade route. These cities/actions appear publicly as clues, so players must infer ownership from later products, trade routes, attacks, and private guesses.
- AI seats can score hand-card candidates, anonymously play eligible cards, buy cards from accessible monster-supply regions, privately discard low-value normal cards when a purchase would exceed the hand limit, participate in simultaneous-play bidding, respond to contract signing windows, infer city owners from product clues, wager on anonymous card ownership, plan monster-lure cards against high-value competing cities, maintain an economic focus product, and switch between grow-focus, defend-routes, and disrupt-competitors strategic intents. AI card scoring includes a generic field-based layer plus playability and hand-pressure metadata, so future cards with cash, GDP derivative, production/transport/consumption, route damage/repair, draw, access, damage, or market-pressure fields receive a baseline utility score even before bespoke AI logic is added. Each AI also tracks a game phase (`opening`, `midgame`, `endgame`) and competitive posture (`leader`, `contesting`, `trailing`): opening AI prioritizes first summons, cities, and basic economy; midgame AI strengthens product routes and starts selective disruption; endgame leaders bias toward defense/cash while trailing AI more strongly values sabotage, short-GDP cards, and attacks on leading cities. These AI route, pressure, scoring, and learning structures are internal opponent logic. Player-facing pages show only public outcomes—cities, products, GDP movement, routes, weather, anonymous card aftermath, monster cash clues, and revealed ownership tags.
- Each player has private owner-guess annotations for unknown cities. Switching player views switches to that player's own annotations.
- Each city starts with one produced alien good and one demanded good.
- Area contract cards require the player to click/select a supply region and a demand region before play. The first five-second card window only publicly displays those preselected endpoints, product, reward, and penalty; after that reveal, the target owner receives a separate five-second accept/reject window; it persists for that player without blocking other cards, and timeout counts as rejection. Current contract families cover selected-product, fixed-product, automatic-match, multi-product replacement, and punitive refusal terms.
- City demand creates trade routes from other cities or land production zones. Destroying a region on an existing route creates logistics disruption and reduces affected city income.
- Product overlap with rival cities creates customer competition and reduces the city's current GDP/min.
- Surviving cities produce funds continuously: current GDP/min is converted into cash every second. Global market refreshes happen every 30-60 seconds and publicly recompute product prices from supply, demand, contract pressure, and disrupted routes; each refresh also records a public city GDP trend with current GDP/min, change from the previous snapshot, recent path, and a short driver/pressure summary. This makes monster damage, broken routes, competition, and supply-demand changes readable as economic consequences without revealing hidden owners. Destroyed cities stop producing cashflow, and surviving cities add asset value at final scoring.
- News is no longer a passive world timer. Public news effects only happen when a player anonymously plays a news card, such as heat manipulation, trending pushes, crisis alerts, market rumors, or regulatory storms. These cards can raise region heat, add public city clues, create short-term supply/demand pressure, or damage routes; the acting player remains hidden like every other card play.
- Planet weather is a public environmental system. The top status bar and a compact strip above the map forecast the next weather event about 60-180 seconds before it arrives; the strip keeps current weather, upcoming weather, affected districts, source, duration, and production/transport/consumption multipliers visible without opening a menu. Each event affects 1-5 districts depending on planet size and then modifies affected districts while active. Weather-control cards can anonymously rewrite the next forecast around a selected district, creating strategic windows for building, route defense, GDP speculation, monster redirection, or city suppression.
- If all regions are destroyed before or during the final countdown, the run ends immediately and settles by final money.
- Commercial scale, product competition, trade-route load, and each monster's resource preference make districts more attractive to autonomous monsters, giving players clues and reasons to redirect attacks. If one district contains several goods a monster wants, those resource matches stack and can attract multiple monsters to the same place.
- Monsters damage region HP through four routes: movement crush along their spherical path, resource drain when a favored good is present, named combat actions from their action tables, and knockback collision damage when a monster is thrown across or into districts. Destroyed regions and cities still resolve through the shared region HP/destruction rules.
- When autonomous monsters meet within encounter range, they choose a usable named action from their own action table—such as 龙车, 甩尾, 光线, or 撞击—so combat appears as text actions with damage and knockback instead of only visual movement.
- Players also interact through paid district card acquisition and one-shot card plays.
- Cards do not charge. A submitted one-shot card immediately leaves the player's hand when it enters the anonymous track, then resolves once after its public display. Persistent bound monster skills stay outside the normal hand limit and remain available after use, entering cooldown instead.
- Cards are bought from the selected district's supply only when it is in the current player's accessible monster-supply range. By default that means a monster's current or adjacent region: the current region grants a 20% discount and an adjacent region charges the rank-I base price. Opening a region's supply window snapshots access and price at that instant; if the monster leaves while the player is choosing a card, that open window can still complete the purchase. Purchases are field actions and do not consume normal action cooldown. Role/card-access effects can extend purchasing to two-hop or global supply with higher multipliers, but they do not extend monster-card summon range. Reacquiring an owned card upgrades it through rank IV with no acquisition limit. New normal cards are limited by a five-card normal hand cap: if buying one would exceed the cap, the buyer must privately discard an old normal card before the purchase completes. Hand size, discard choice, and discarded-card identity are private information.
- Card prices are based on the rank-I effect's power and display as base, advanced, high, or flagship tiers. Ranks II-IV keep the rank-I price. Player economy tracking records build spend, card spend, card income, current-refresh cashflow, and recent cash movement.
- Card UI now derives a short strategy route, use explanation, and strength budget from each card's data fields. The current route labels include city growth, city suppression, financial speculation, contract bargaining, intelligence inference, monster routes, supply/deckbuilding, combat damage, monster lure pressure, news information warfare, and weather bargaining. The strength budget shows a band such as base-use, efficiency, route-core, or terminal pressure, plus main power drivers and counterplay/gating points. Card detail pages use a TCG-style structure: card face, positioning, cost/gates, core effect, key fields, I-IV upgrade gradient, and anonymous resolution presentation.
- Playing a card normally requires a specified product and city-flow amount, but does not consume the product. Some cards additionally cost money. All card plays are anonymous public events.
- Public logs and map callouts show the card, target, and result but not the player who used it. During a live run, each player only sees their own exact cash, city assets, cashflow, cash path, ledger, hand size, hand faces, and discard records; rival economies and rival hands stay private and must be inferred from public actions, product-flow requirements, bids, damage clues, and map changes. Final scoring can reveal exact settlement values.
- The economy overview includes the current player's inference board. It summarizes that player's private city-owner annotations, public card-owner and monster-owner reveals, and recent anonymous card play requirements. Card requirements are compared only against the current player's own visible product flow; the board does not validate private guesses, scan rival economies, or expose hidden cash/ownership that has not been publicly revealed.
- The intel dossier is a dedicated inference branch. It explains that city-owner intelligence becomes final money, while card-owner guesses settle immediately as private transfers; then it groups private city marks, each mark's low/medium/high confidence and reason, anonymous card betting status, monster damage cash clues, and public city clue history for the current player without validating guesses before settlement. It also ranks city leads by investigation priority from GDP, competition, disrupted routes, public clues, unmarked state, and confidence; lets the current player set or clear private city-owner marks directly; adjust mark confidence/reason; and jump to related region, card, monster, product, or economy pages before returning directly to the dossier.
- Economy cards can raise or short a selected product's current price, reduce its volatility, upgrade a city's lowest-level product, shift a city's product line, redesign city demand, damage public city trade routes, insure and repair the active player's routes, add sustained product contracts through cards such as 远期采购, 期货套保, and 包销协议, add temporary city order/contract income through cards such as 短期订单, 军需临单, and 星际会展, or anonymously buy a selected city's GDP rising/falling through 城市买涨 and 城市做空 cards. GDP derivative cards record the city's current instantaneous GDP as a baseline, hold for the card's listed real-time duration, pay at expiry when GDP has moved in the chosen direction, and clear/settle down-side positions when the city is destroyed.
- Economy overview shows the recent cash path, tracked-window net change, and public city GDP trend text, making expansion costs, card cashflow, recurring city income, and damage-driven GDP drops visible as a progression rather than only a final balance. The main play screen keeps those details out of the hand area.
- Cards that require a monster target pause before they enter the public queue and ask the player to choose a target monster. Once a card is submitted, it does not resolve immediately: the first card in an empty stack opens a 0.5-second simultaneous-play window.
- If only one card appears during that window, it goes into its own 5-second anonymous public display and then resolves. If multiple players submit cards during the window, all submitted cards pause for one 5-second anonymous auction; bids are public amounts, but bidder and card-owner identities remain hidden.
- After the auction closes, the whole batch order is locked once by bid amount, with equal bids ordered clockwise from the relevant reference player. The locked batch never reopens its auction. Cards submitted after its 0.5-second intake closes or during its displays are accepted into a separate next-batch waiting area instead of being rejected. When the current batch clears, all waiting cards form one new batch and open only one new five-second auction; if only one card is waiting, it goes directly to its own five-second display.
- Each card with a locked bid privately pays that bid to the previous resolved card's owner when its display begins. This can cross the boundary between batches when cards waited behind an active batch; if no previous card exists, no tip is paid. The public can see anonymous bid/card activity, not the payer or recipient, unless later inference or owner reveal makes that clear.
- The top anonymous card track records history, current display cards, and pending cards. Players can drag the track, select any card, and guess which player played it. A correct guess transfers the stake from the true owner to the guesser and attaches a public owner label to that card; a wrong guess privately transfers the stake to the true owner without revealing the label. Each anonymous card keeps the play-requirement snapshot from submission time, so the lobby, full-screen reveal, and track can show the public product-flow/cash condition as inference evidence even if the selected trade product changes later. Resolved cards also retain public auction clues such as whether their locked tip paid the previous anonymous card's owner; the clue is visible on the track and economy overview without naming the payer or recipient.
- Direct monster commands from cards are one-time effects only; they do not grant ongoing control of that monster. Lure cards such as 诱导电波 specify a target monster and temporarily point only that monster's next automatic movement toward the current selected district; after that movement or stop resolves, the lure marker disappears.
- The active player panel keeps a single short next-action hint on screen, such as first summoning a monster, building the first city, buying from a monster-supply region, responding to a contract, choosing a monster target, or protecting/attacking during the final countdown. The first minutes of a run also show a dismissible checklist for first summon, city building, card buying, anonymous card play, and the economy overview shortcut; its next-step card is split into action, reason, and entry so players can act without reading long rules, and it only checks off the economy overview after that player actually opens it. Longer rules remain in menu branches.
- Final settlement now automatically opens a post-run settlement page and also writes a compact run summary to the log and standings page: winner, settlement money, top city-income source, top card/intel income source, role income, key resolved cards, monster cash/damage impact, a note that AI internal plans stay hidden, per-player final money and income-source breakdowns, surviving cities, destroyed regions, and the current best city snapshot. The post-run page links directly to standings, the economy overview, and new-run setup.
- Starter monster cards are unrestricted for the opening summon. Later monster cards carry their own summon-region text, including generic monster-zone, land monster-zone, or ocean monster-zone requirements.
- Monster cards have ranks I-IV. Rank I uses the base automatic-action table; ranks II-IV increasingly shift probability weight toward later high-risk skills, while still leaving the monster autonomous.
- Monster movement, card range, AOE, and knockback are measured in meters on a spherical world surface.
- The player sees a flat local projection when zoomed in and a globe in space when zoomed out.
- The map can display the transport path for a selected product, with disrupted routes highlighted.
- Monster actions, special actions, failed movement, and major reactions appear as text callouts on the map.
- City construction rises visibly from the map; realtime city income and city destruction also appear as anonymous map callouts without revealing ownership.

## Current UI branches

- 根菜单入口: the root menu is no longer a rigid vertical button list. Its branch entries are grouped under section headers and rendered through reusable responsive card grids that can reflow across 1-3 columns, preserving hover/pressed styles and callbacks while making future large layout and art-direction changes safer.
- 菜单快捷导航: every menu branch keeps top chips for 开局、局势、经济、情报、规则、图鉴. The current branch is disabled as a location cue, while the other chips jump directly to their pages so players do not need to back out through the root menu.
- 菜单交互提示: every menu page now keeps a compact interaction strip under the breadcrumb. The strip is generated from shared page-state logic, so root menus, setup, rules, economy, intel, and codex pages can be visually rearranged later while still telling players what to hover, when thumbnails support double-click detail entry, how previous/next detail navigation works, and how to return to thumbnail pages or parent branches.
- 游戏规则: temporary dense reference page for full rules and controls before a later tutorial exists.
- 经济总览: live current-run dashboard for product hot/cold rankings, active economy weather, route-income prospects with city income breakdowns and GDP trends, recent public situation changes, anonymous card aftermath, clue summaries, and the current player's private cash/assets/income/path/ledger; rival economy entries, rival hand sizes, AI route/pressure buckets, and discard records are hidden until final settlement.
- 情报档案: current-player inference dossier for setting city-owner marks plus their confidence and reason, ranking city investigation priority, card-owner betting state, monster cash clues, recent city public clues, and the exact way intelligence converts back into money without revealing hidden owners early; includes clue jump buttons into related codex/economy pages.
- 图鉴: unified read-only compendium that opens 角色图鉴, 怪兽生态档案, 卡牌图鉴, 商品图鉴, and 区域图鉴.
- 角色图鉴: alien syndicate role-card details with procedural temporary card faces, species traits, and mechanical passives. It explicitly keeps starter monster selection independent from roles. The main game hand area no longer shows the role card; it stays compact for hand/bid/target interaction.
- 开局准备: main-menu branch that previews 3-8 total seats, 2-7 AI opponents, roguelike depth I-VI, lets each player cycle/select an alien role card, shows mechanical passives, starter monster card faces, first-summon access, bound-skill rewards, and the monster landing card-buying radius before the player explicitly confirms a new run.
- 首召引导: while no monster is on the field, the active player panel shows the current landing district and a direct "在选区首召" action for the starter monster card.
- 开局轻引导: during the first 1-2 minutes, the active player panel shows a short dismissible checklist for summoning, city building, monster-supply card buying, anonymous card play, and opening the economy overview. It also shows the current next step, stores whether the current player actually opened the economy overview, and persists both that progress and the dismissed state in the saved run.
- 怪兽生态档案: responsive thumbnail album for field-monster behavior, not a duplicate monster-card catalog. Hover or single-click shows a detail preview, double-click opens the full detail page, and only that detail page uses previous/next monster navigation plus a return-to-thumbnails button. Detail pages show temporary monster art, action descriptions, action probabilities, resource focus, positive economy weather, movement speed, move-crush damage, resource-drain damage, and knockback-collision damage; the corresponding monster summon card links back to 卡牌图鉴.
- 卡牌图鉴: one shared card compendium with monster, economy, business/contract, combat/command, intel, news-event, weather-control, supply/lure, and other subcategories. It opens as a responsive thumbnail album; hover or single-click shows a concise detail preview, double-click opens the full detail page, and only that detail page uses previous/next card navigation plus a return-to-thumbnails button. Detail pages show every card's effect, field-derived strategy route/use, strength budget, Roman-numeral rank, target requirement, one-shot or persistent rule, HP/movement/field-duration/summon-region facts for monster cards, rank-I reference price, a TCG-style I-IV upgrade gradient, and the shared card face.
- 商品图鉴: responsive thumbnail album for products. Hover or single-click shows a preview with product price tiers, current and recent price paths, volatility, supply/demand pressure, route-disruption pressure, sustained product contracts, active product/route-flow economy weather, and product-filtered city clue history; double-click opens the detail page, and only that detail page uses previous/next product navigation plus a return-to-thumbnails button.
- 区域图鉴: generated region terrain, HP/damage, latest damage source, route load, monster attraction reasons, public economy information, city income breakdowns, GDP trend text, recent anonymous city clues, card choices, neighbors, city temporary contracts, city route-flow acceleration, and city public status without revealing hidden owners.

## Current placeholder art policy

Card faces, role faces, monster-card motifs, monster portraits, and the project icon are procedural temporary art. They are intentionally routed through reusable UI components/assets so authored art can replace them later without rewriting the rules logic.
