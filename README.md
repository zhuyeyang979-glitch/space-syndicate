# 太空辛迪加 / Space Syndicate

Godot 4 project for **太空辛迪加 / Space Syndicate**, based on the local rules draft and art requirement list.

## Current Prototype Scope

- 3-8 seat PVE roguelike sandbox with a lightweight main/pause menu. Each run can include 2-7 AI opponents plus at least one human/local player seat.
- Continuous real-time clock with pause for menus/rules inspection. Card play, auction windows, monster actions, cashflow, futures, and GDP movement are tuned around live time rather than player-facing time-multiplier presets.
- News, market, and monster pressure now use one fixed real-time balance profile. The normal player UI exposes no pacing presets, forced monster/event advance buttons, or manual settlement shortcut.
- The main menu's 开局准备 branch previews total seats, AI opponent count, roguelike challenge depth, per-player public alien role-card selection, optional random AI roles, each independent starter monster card face, and a first-summon summary showing unrestricted access, bound-skill rewards, and the card-buying radius before confirming a new run. A confirmed new run begins with no monsters on the planet. Twenty-four selectable alien syndicate role cards now resolve without duplicates and provide working positive passives: opening cash, per-minute cashflow from a named product, a free extra regional card, cash when an owned monster upgrades, private intel reveals/traces, owner-guess modifiers, contract-flow discounts, expanded card acquisition range, or a public +1 monster/military control limit. Role cards do not change starter monster HP, speed, duration, or bound-skill count. Later monster cards can summon, upgrade, or refresh monsters while respecting the active owned-monster limit, and print their own HP, movement, field duration, and summon-region restriction on the card; different monsters can require generic, land, or ocean monster-zone landing rules. There is no persistent directly player-controlled monster: all monsters auto-move and auto-act.
- A confirmed new run starts with an empty planet; field monsters appear only from the starter or later monster cards that players actually play.
- While the field has no monsters, the active player's panel shows a first-summon prompt with the current landing district and a direct "在选区首召" action for the starter monster card.
- The main game view is intentionally minimal: a large planet map, the anonymous card track, and the active player's hand. The hand header only keeps compact player/cash/bid context for the current local player; rival cash, hand size, hand faces, and discard records remain private. Role details, city economy, cash paths, ledgers, and region facts live in the menu/codex/overview branches.
- The map uses a spherical world model. Zooming out shows the planet in space with region boundaries projected onto the globe; zooming in shows a local XY-style flat projection of the same spherical surface. A map button opens the map in fullscreen.
- The root main menu now behaves like a compact digital-board-game table lobby: one central planet medallion, three primary commands (开新一桌, 继续牌桌, 资料库), and a small utility rail for 游戏规则, 读取局面, and 退出游戏. In-game analysis branches such as 局势排名, 经济总览, and 情报档案 stay inside the live/pause/table context instead of crowding the root lobby. 资料库 opens the unified 图鉴 branch with submenus for 角色图鉴, 怪兽生态档案, 卡牌图鉴, 商品图鉴, and 区域图鉴.
- The menu can save and load the current run locally, not only setup preferences.
- The read-only 怪兽生态档案 shows each field monster's actions, action probabilities, movement speed, linked monster card, fixed skills, resource focus, positive economy weather, move-crush damage, knockback-collision damage, and procedural temporary monster art. It opens as a responsive thumbnail album with hover/single-click previews, double-click detail entry, and detail-only previous/next navigation; monster cards themselves stay inside the shared 卡牌图鉴.
- The 角色图鉴 shows all alien syndicate role cards with procedural temporary card faces, species traits, and working positive passives while keeping starter monster choice independent. Its card stat line exposes the concrete cash/product/card/upgrade/intel/range trigger instead of leaving the benefit only in prose. The single shared 卡牌图鉴 includes monster cards rather than splitting them into a separate branch, and filters cards into monster, economy, business/contract, combat/command, intel, supply/lure, and other subcategories. It opens as a responsive thumbnail album: the grid size adapts to viewport space, hovering or single-clicking a card shows a concise detail preview, double-clicking enters a TCG-style full detail page, and the full detail page alone exposes previous/next card navigation plus a return-to-thumbnails action. 商品图鉴 follows the same album pattern: it opens as a responsive thumbnail grid whose product cards already show price, supply/demand pressure, and a primary public strategy tag before opening detail; hover/single-click previews price, active weather, strategy-route pressure, futures/warehouse exposure, monster preference, related cards, and product-filtered city clues; double-click enters a TCG-style product detail page with market, strategy, finance/weather, ecology/card, map-entry, clue, and rule-hint sections, and detail-only previous/next navigation can return to thumbnails. 区域图鉴 shows public region details, HP/damage, latest monster damage source, route load, monster attraction reasons, local goods, available paid cards, city temporary contracts, city route-flow acceleration, and city public status without revealing hidden owners.
- Players switch active operator views, secretly urbanize empty districts, privately mark who they think owns an unknown city, claim a card from the selected district, or play a card from hand.
- Player actions now use short cooldowns so real-time play has pacing instead of button spamming.
- Cards do not charge. District cards are normally bought with player funds only from a monster's current region or an adjacent region; the current region costs 80% and adjacent regions use the I-rank base price. Role/card-access effects can extend purchase range to two-hop or global supply with a higher multiplier, but do not relax monster-card summon restrictions. If buying a new normal card would exceed the five-card normal hand limit, the buyer privately discards an old normal card before the purchase completes; hand size, discard choice, and discarded card identity are not public. A submitted non-persistent card leaves the hand as soon as it enters the anonymous track, then resolves once after public display and can be reacquired from district supply.
- Reacquiring a card already owned upgrades it when a next rank exists.
- Card prices form a gradient by the rank-I effect's economic power, with explicit base/advanced/high/flagship tiers and a district-access discount. Ranks I-IV all retain the rank-I purchase price. Card faces and codex pages now also show a field-derived strength budget: a budget band, the main power drivers, and the counterplay/gating points that keep stronger cards readable and answerable. Player panels track build spend, card spend, card income, realtime cashflow, and recent cash movement.
- Playing a card normally checks a required product and flow amount supplied by the player's cities without consuming that product. Some cards also require an extra cash payment; monster cards can add a cash surcharge based on how many monsters are already on the field. Cards that require a monster target ask for that target after the player chooses to play them; direct player-interaction cards ask for a target player before entering the anonymous card lane.
- Economy and interaction cards support price pumping, short selling, market stabilization, city-product upgrades, city product-line shifts, demand redesign, route sabotage, supply-chain insurance, route repair/flow acceleration through 应急修复, rival production/transport/consumption suppression through 竞争封锁, private clue pursuit through 线索悬赏, route-weather forecasting through 航线预报, direct hand pressure through 星链拆解 and 影仓牵引, temporary city ownership disputes through 产权冻结, broad city suppression through 轨道齐射, product-growth catalysts, sustained product contracts such as 远期采购, 期货套保, and 包销协议, temporary city order/contract deals such as 短期订单, 军需临单, and 星际会展, plus commodity futures/warehouse stockpiles and 城市买涨/城市做空 GDP derivative cards. Commodity futures show anonymous product-level direction/expiry pressure; warehouse stockpiles also leave public city status with product, unit count, and expiry without revealing the owner. Stockpiled cities feed event targeting, monster attraction, resource preference matching, city short-GDP targeting, orbital-barrage target sorting, military deploy/order scoring, and hidden AI attack/defense scoring, so a warehouse is a visible financial opportunity and a real strategic liability. The economy overview and intel dossier now surface anonymous warehouse targets with product, units, expiry, GDP/min, and counterplay hints while keeping owner identity, cash, hands, and AI routes private. GDP derivative cards hold for real seconds, then settle against the city's instantaneous GDP movement. These cards change current prices, volatility, product lists, demand lists, product levels, route damage, sustained product demand/supply pressure, product growth multipliers, city logistics multipliers, temporary route-flow windows, temporary contract income, region-damage GDP penalties, temporary ownership-dispute penalties, and later city income rather than acting as flavor-only cards.
- Military-force cards live in the same shared card pool rather than a separate unit layer. The current prototype includes short-lived defense forces, air fighters, orbital bombers, heavy tanks, missile batteries, submarine fleets, and sea warships. They are fully controlled through private reusable command skills instead of autonomous monster AI: move, guard, strike a district, or attack a monster. Air, land, and ocean forces use terrain-aware deployment and movement multipliers. Their movement does not crush buildings like monster travel, but strikes or armed presence can create short GDP pressure and route pressure that appears in city income breakdowns. AI seats now evaluate both military-force deployment and reusable military commands from fields and map pressure, so they can deploy for guard, rival-strike, monster-intercept, route-control, or terrain-staging roles, then command units to guard damaged/high-GDP owned cities, strike rival logistics or warehouse cities, attack resource-matched monster threats, and reposition by terrain and route value without exposing those hidden scores to the player.
- Summoned monsters can apply positive economy weather to their preferred products while they are part of the run, such as faster positive price growth or faster commercial flow on related routes. Temporary cards can stack above that weather for their listed seconds/minutes, and product-contract cards add visible sustained demand/supply pressure with their own countdown.
- Player panels display a compact recent cash path, tracked-window net change, and a recent economy ledger so the run's economic gradient remains visible while spending, card income, battle rewards, commercial actions, and city income accumulate.
- Cards that need a monster target pause before entering the shared lane and ask for a target after the player presses play. One-shot lure cards such as 诱导电波 can point the chosen monster's next automatic movement toward the current selected district, but the monster returns to its own probability table immediately afterward. Every submitted card then enters the anonymous card-resolution system: the first card in an empty lane opens a 0.5-second simultaneous-play window, a lone card proceeds to a 5-second public display, and multiple cards open one 5-second anonymous auction before the whole batch order locks.
- Anonymous card auctions show bid amounts without revealing bidders or owners. The locked batch resolves in bid/clockwise order and does not reopen auctions mid-batch. Cards submitted after the 0.5-second intake closes or while that batch is displaying are accepted into a visible next-batch waiting area; once the current batch clears, all waiting cards enter one new auction together (or a lone waiting card goes straight to its own display). A locked bid is privately paid to the previous resolved card's owner when one exists, including across a batch boundary.
- The top card track records anonymous history/current/pending cards, supports horizontal dragging, and lets the current player wager on a card's owner. Correct guesses publicly attach the owner label to that card; wrong guesses pay the true owner privately without revealing the label.
- Public card events show the card, target, and result but never name the player who played it unless a later correct owner guess reveals that card's owner label. During live play, the economy overview exposes only the current player's exact cash, assets, income, cash path, and ledger; rival economies remain hidden until final settlement. The 情报档案 branch collects the current player's city-owner marks, per-mark confidence and reason, card-owner betting state, monster cash clues, and recent city public clues without revealing hidden truth early; it ranks city leads by investigation priority, offers direct private city-mark buttons, and jumps into the related region, card, monster, product, or economy pages.
- The selected district's current supply choice is kept compact on the main screen, while hand cards and codex entries use the full shared card art.
- Roguelike-style generated world map: challenge depth I-VI scales the spherical planet from a small early-run board of 6-9 regions to larger worlds with dozens of irregular land/ocean regions, currently up to roughly 40-54 regions, and also raises the cash goal used as the run's roguelike target. The run objective is deliberately money-shaped: survive the chaos, protect income, disrupt rivals, and earn enough settlement cash to push toward bigger planets. When any player's visible estimated settlement money reaches that depth's cash goal, a saved 60-second final countdown starts anonymously; the countdown does not cancel if the trigger player falls back under the line, and the game then settles by highest final money. Each region supports damage, transport, local cards, and where valid urbanization.
- Land and ocean regions initially produce one terrain-appropriate good and have one local demand. Ocean regions can provide fish, algae/fiber, subsea oil, coral/shell materials, offshore crystal, tidal energy, and other marine products while also serving as lower-cost shipping lanes. Product-specific cards are filtered by the goods that exist in the current run.
- Urbanizing a land district creates a visible building cluster with one initial produced good and one initial demanded good. The owner is only revealed in that player's view; rivals see an anonymous city plus their own private guess marker.
- Area contract cards require the player to click/select a supply region and a demand region before play. The first five-second card window only publicly displays those preselected endpoints, product, reward, and penalty; after that reveal, the target city's true owner gets a separate five-second accept/reject window that remains on their screen without blocking other card plays; timeout counts as rejection. After the decision resolves, the anonymous card track and economy aftermath show the contract result, connected districts, product, and reward/penalty summary while keeping both the acting player and responding owner hidden. The current pool includes selected-product, fixed 环晶电池, automatic-match, multi-product replacement, and punitive refusal contract families.
- Every 30-60 seconds, the global market refresh can let AI syndicates secretly urbanize profitable empty land. These new city clusters appear publicly as anonymous buildings, spend AI funds, and give the active player new ownership mysteries to mark and infer.
- AI syndicates also perform anonymous commercial actions around the live economy and global refreshes: they can create demand/supply pressure around products they benefit from or sabotage competing cities' trade routes. They score hand-card candidates, anonymously play monster/economy/contract/direct-interaction/military-force/military-command cards when requirements are met, buy cards from accessible monster-supply regions, privately discard low-value normal cards when a purchase would exceed the hand limit, participate in simultaneous-play bidding with budgeted tips, evaluate five-second phase-response windows for counter cards, respond to anonymous contract signing windows, infer city owners from product clues, wager on anonymous card ownership, choose public monster-wager sides and stake size from combat odds plus hidden ownership/city-risk interests, steer monster-lure cards toward high-value product-overlapping competing cities, use direct interaction against likely leaders or exposed hand/city targets, deploy military units for guard/strike/intercept/route-control roles, command military units to guard, strike, hunt monsters, or reposition, maintain an economic focus product, and switch between grow-focus, defend-routes, and disrupt-competitors strategic intents. Human card-supply windows snapshot monster access at open time, so a card purchase can finish even if the monster moves away during selection; one player can only keep one such region supply window. Card play/buy scoring includes a generic effect-field layer plus hand-pressure/playability fields so future cards with cash, GDP derivative, production/transport/consumption, route damage/repair, route-flow multipliers, intel trace/city reveal/contract trace, weather-control, draw, access, damage, direct hand/city interaction, military force/command, or market-pressure fields can receive baseline AI utility before bespoke logic is written. Commodity futures add a dedicated field-driven scorer: AI reads product-bet direction, duration, multiplier, stockpile units, and warehouse requirements to choose long, short, or port-warehouse plans without hardcoding card names. Direct-interaction cards add hidden AI metadata for target role/score, visible settlement gap, public card-owner signal, hand effect pressure, city GDP, warehouse pressure, route damage, and expected barrage damage, so 拆牌、牵牌、产权冻结和轨道齐射 can pressure leaders or strategic financial/logistics targets without exposing AI pressure buckets. Contract responses add hidden AI metadata for route match, accept/reject value, response margin, decline risk, target GDP/route damage, source owner, and accept/decline economic delta, letting AI sign punitive or route-building contracts and reject rival-enabling contracts from card fields. Counter responses add hidden AI metadata for target resolution, target card, threat score, opportunity cost, counter strength, conversion from monster card, phase posture, and reason key; the public still only sees that an anonymous counter canceled the revealed card. Weather-control cards add hidden AI metadata for type, role, covered city count, route load, terrain bonus, focus-product bonus, own-route value, and rival-suppression value, so AI can use gravity tides to protect/accelerate its own logistics or acid/fog/storm windows to pressure rival GDP. Military force and command cards add dedicated metadata for deploy role/score/terrain/route pressure/monster risk/future deploy district plus command type, role, score, distance, bound unit, and unit type. Monster wagers add hidden AI scoring metadata for score, confidence, owner bias, city bias, and expected damage while the public still sees only bettor identity, target, and amount. Above those layers, each AI now keeps a product-route plan that progresses through build supply, create demand, strengthen route, defend route, and attack rival stages. Concrete supply/demand/flow progress preserves the incumbent plan; only a meaningfully stronger alternative can displace an established route, and create-demand applies a hard stage-mismatch penalty to pure supply cards while the plan is still missing demand. Build, card play/buy, counters, weather, contracts, business, bidding, strategy, route, military, monster-wager, and intel candidates now use per-seat learned policy bonuses from both realtime cashflow/global-refresh rewards and final roguelike money/rank rewards, tagged by action, policy, strategy, route stage, product, and target player where relevant.
- Every global market refresh, the product market recomputes current prices from supply, demand, contract pressure, and disrupted routes. Surviving cities continuously earn cash from production, fulfilled demand routes, and transit using their current GDP/min. Rival cities that sell the same products compete for customers and reduce one another's GDP/min.
- City income is visible as a breakdown of production income, fulfilled-route income, transit income, permanent bonuses, temporary contract income, competition penalties, route-disruption penalties, and region-damage GDP penalties in the region codex and economy overview. Global market snapshots also show a public GDP trend with latest change and driver/pressure text, so monster damage, route disruption, and supply-demand shifts can be read as economic movement.
- Players can show a selected product's trade routes on the map. Destroying regions along an existing route creates shipping disruption that reduces affected city income.
- Destroyed cities stop earning. At settlement, surviving value and intelligence results are converted into money; victory is decided only by final money. If all regions are destroyed before the cash-goal countdown resolves, the game also ends immediately.
- The standings menu previews current score, active city assets, potential GDP/min, and cumulative business income.
- The economy overview menu summarizes product hot/cold rankings, active economy weather, route-income prospects, and the current player's private cash/assets/income/path/ledger while hiding rival economy totals until final settlement. The intel dossier explains how guesses convert to final money and groups the evidence players use to decide which cities, cards, and monsters likely belong to whom; its clue buttons can open the matching codex page and return directly to the dossier.
- A local run save stores the generated map, players, cities, cards, monsters, timers, logs, and scoring state for later continuation.
- Monster movement, card ranges, AOE, and knockback use meter distances on the spherical surface, not grid steps.
- Monster markers use procedural temporary portrait tokens, animate between world positions, and leave movement cues so recent movement is visible at a glance.
- Monster actions appear as text callouts on the map instead of being represented only by movement. Resource matches can stack, so a district with several attractive goods can pull multiple monsters together.
- Monster cards use Roman-numeral ranks I-IV. Higher-rank summoned monsters keep their card's HP, movement, and duration rules, and their automatic action weights tilt toward later, more dangerous named actions.
- Monsters now damage region HP through movement crush, resource drain, combat actions, and knockback collisions. Region damage feeds back into city GDP. Flying movement skips path-crush damage, while aquatic monsters can use ocean/land speed multipliers. When monsters meet within encounter range they use named actions from their own action table; knockback can damage the route/landing regions and destroy cities through the same region HP track.
- Monster encounters can trigger a public monster wager before the combat action resolves. The game clock freezes for up to 30 seconds, every player must publicly bet at least ¥100 on one participating monster, and bettor identity/target/amount are visible clues. AI seats evaluate expected monster damage from the action table, current damage, HP/armor/rank, ownership interest, nearby city risk, and resource matches; high-confidence AI can publicly raise to ¥500, so the size of a bet is also a clue. Multi-monster fights in one district become multiway wager pools; after betting closes or everyone decides, the delayed attack resolves and winning bettors split the total pot by winning-stake share.
- Card and skill ranges are meter-based AOE/range checks, so knockback, pursuit, and explosions should be tuned in meters.
- News cards can raise district heat or create public market pressure, while active cities, contested products, trade-route load, and monster resource preferences shape monster attention.
- Procedural temporary UI art for cards and monsters, with final authored assets still tracked in `docs/art_requirements.md`.

## Run

Open this folder in Godot 4.x and run `scenes/main.tscn`.

On Windows, `Launch Space Syndicate.cmd` tries to find Godot from PATH, `GODOT_EXE`, the sibling workspace `tools/godot-*` folder, or common install locations, then opens this project. `Launch Space Syndicate Prototype.cmd` is kept as a compatibility alias. If Godot is not installed yet, the launcher opens the project folder instead.

## Keyboard Shortcuts

- `1`-`8`: select player/seat, based on the configured player count
- `Q` / `E`: select previous/next district
- `B`: urbanize the selected empty district
- `G`: cycle the player-owner guess for the selected city
- `M`: save or clear the active player's private owner guess
- `R`: show or hide the selected product's trade routes
- `T`: cycle the visible trade-route product
- `C`: cycle the selected district supply card
- `X`: claim the selected district card
- `Space`: pause/resume
- `Esc`: open/close menu

The project is intentionally data-light and UI-driven so that later balancing and art replacement can happen without rebuilding the scene tree by hand.
